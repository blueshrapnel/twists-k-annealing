\begin{abstract}
% DRAFT
Evolving per-state action relabellings for multi-goal navigation is
dominated by fitness evaluation: every genome scores as one planning solve
per goal.  We show that a two-stage schedule --- evolve on a random
$10\%$ goal subsample, then consolidate with a short full-goal tail seeded
from the stage-one population --- finds \emph{better} twists than
full-goal evolution at a fraction of the cost, at every environment and
scale tested.  At $13 \times 13$ the annealed runs beat a five-times-budget
full-goal baseline at $28\%$ of its evaluation cost; at $15 \times 15$,
where fixed-budget full-goal search starves, annealing recovers the homing
structure the baselines lose.  The subsampled stage finds the same
attractor objects, not merely comparable fitness: the consolidation tail
inherits and completes the stage-one basins in place.  Full-goal elite
re-evaluation, the obvious de-noising alternative, is actively harmful.
\end{abstract}
